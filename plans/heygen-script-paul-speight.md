# HeyGen script — Paul Speight introduces Phoenix Watch (v2)

~75 seconds. Dry, mildly comedic. Suit/branding = one passing line only.
Slogan lands near the end, tied to the t-shirt.

---

Hello. I'm Paul Speight — sponsor, Lloyds Banking Group. The suit? Borrowed. The enthusiasm? Entirely my own.

I'm here to tell you about something we built called Phoenix Watch — built live, in a couple of hours, by describing what we wanted in plain English. That's vibe coding. No procurement. No eighteen-month programme. No steering committee with its own steering committee.

Here's the problem it solves. The FCA fines a firm. The firm quietly dissolves. And its directors — like the mythical phoenix, or like me at a free buffet — simply rise again, running brand-new companies by Tuesday.

Phoenix Watch cross-references three hundred real FCA fines against Companies House — all public data — and draws the network live: the fined firm, its directors, and every new company they've popped up in since. Red nodes are the ones that should make you spill your tea. Each director gets a transparent risk score — same address, same line of business, suspiciously fresh incorporation date — all the working shown, no black box.

And because it's twenty-twenty-six, you can simply ask it. "Which director here worries you most?" — and the AI investigates live, pulling new records and growing the graph in front of you, with names, company numbers and dates. It cites its sources. Which is more than I do in most meetings.

So the message — it's on the t-shirts, and it's the whole idea in one line: you can dissolve the company. You can't dissolve the data.

Built in hours. From public data. By talking to a computer. If that doesn't mildly terrify and delight you in equal measure — you may already work in change management.

Enjoy the demo.
